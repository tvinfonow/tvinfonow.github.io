Wafaa Bilal (Arabic wafaa bll (Arabic: wf ba:l Born June 10 in 1966) is an Iraqi American artist, a former professor at the School of the Art Institute of Chicago and is currently as an assistant professor in the Tisch School of the Arts at New York University. The most well-known of his works is his project, Domestic Tension, a performance in which the artist stayed in a gallery for one month, and was killed by paintballs from a remote location by Internet users viewing the event via a webcam. Also, he is known in his autobiography, Shoot an Iraqi: The Art, life and Resistance under the Gun, inspired by that performance, which reveals the horrible experiences that come with living in a war zone and being a child under the Saddam Hussein regime.

In May 2007 Bilal began a project lasting 30 days named Domestic Tension to protest his opposition to the Iraq War. In the course of the installation, Bilal confined himself to one room in the FlatFile Galleries, located in Chicago. While the artist was in a small space to a small space, he was able to be viewed 24 hours a day with the camera he linked to Internet. Alongside his camera Bilal installed a remote controlled paintball gun which users could hit him any moment. The gun fired an unpleasant yellow color and released an audio that was as loud as semi-automatic guns each time it fired.

The inspiration for Domestic Tension stemmed from Bilal’s experiences in refugee camps during the rule of Saddam Hussein, losing members of his family in the war, and trying to cope with the reality of war as it raged on the other side of the world. One day, Bilal learned that his people were being killed by soldiers who were not even stationed in Iraq—they had the power to shoot missiles “from an armchair in front of a computer somewhere, as if it were all some kind of video game.” Frightened by how easily soldiers (and Americans) could distance themselves from the terrors of war, Bilal used Domestic Tension as a way to constantly remind himself of the horrors going on in Iraq.
