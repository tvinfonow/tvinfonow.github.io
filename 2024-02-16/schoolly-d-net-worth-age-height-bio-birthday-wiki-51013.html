Schoolly D Biography

Schoolly D is one of the most popular and richest Musician who was born on June 22, 1962 in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, United States. Jesse Bonds Weaver Jr. (born June 22nd 1962) is better known under his stage name Schoolly D (sometimes called Schooly D) is an American rapper born in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania.

In November of 2006, Schoolly D and Cartoon Network were sued for their Aqua Teen Hunger Force tune. A drummer named from Terence Yerves claimed he had composed the music together with Schoolly D during 1999 when in the Meat Locker Studio. Yerves knew the track could be used in an upcoming television show but was not happy with the use of it for Aqua Teen Hunger Force. however, he did not submit the copyright claim with the Library of Congress until May 2006, when the series fourth season had began airing. In the suit, Yerves requested that he be paid $150,000 per time the show shown following the filing of the suit and also demanded all copies of the DVDs be destroyed in order to require Aqua Teen Hunger Force to stop broadcasting.

Schoolly D contributed songs and music to many Abel Ferrara films, including “P.S.K.” and “Saturday Night” (from Saturday Night! – The Album) as well as “King of New York” to Ferrara’s film of the same name and the title track from Am I Black Enough For You? that was played during the climactic shoot-out in that film, the title track from How a Black Man Feels, and “Signifying Rapper” (from Smoke Some Kill), which was used in Ferrara’s film Bad Lieutenant. Because Led Zeppelin successfully sued due to an uncleared interpolation of its song “Kashmir” in “Signifying Rapper”, the song was omitted from the soundtrack of the film and from subsequent releases of the film.

The Beastie Boys sampled Schoolly D’s song “Gucci Time” on their 1986 smash “Time to Get Ill.”

Schoolly D collaborated along with DJ Code Money in the mid-1980s. His lyrics spoke of urban realism, violence and sexual brilliance, making Schoolly D his first hip-hop gangsta (see further below). The rapper is also interviewed in the 1986 documentary Big Fun in the Big Town. He later took on the Afrocentric style and brought Afrocentric culture to hip hop alongside KRS-One.
